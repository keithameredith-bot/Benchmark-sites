<h2 class="text-text-100 mt-3 -mb-1 text-[1.125rem] font-bold">Why Florida Heat Is So Hard on Tires</h2>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Tires are engineered with a mix of rubber, steel belts, and fabric plies held together by heat-activated bonding agents. In cooler climates those bonds stay stable for years. In Central Florida, the combination of:</p>
<ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1 [li_&amp;]:gap-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-1 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Sustained 90°+ daytime temperatures for 5+ months</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">UV exposure that breaks down rubber compounds</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">Road surface temperatures that can exceed 140°F</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2">High humidity that accelerates oxidation</li>
</ul>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">…means a tire in Ocala typically ages <strong>30–40% faster</strong> than the same tire in Ohio or Michigan. Tread depth isn't the only thing that matters here. Age and heat damage can take a tire out of service long before it "wears out."</p>

<h2 class="text-text-100 mt-3 -mb-1 text-[1.125rem] font-bold">7 Warning Signs Your Tires Are Failing</h2>
<h3 class="text-text-100 mt-2 -mb-1 text-base font-bold">1. Cracks in the Sidewall (Dry Rot)</h3>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Look closely at the sidewall — the flat side of the tire facing outward. Small spiderweb cracks, flaking rubber, or deep fissures are dry rot, and it's extremely common on vehicles that sit outside in the Florida sun. <strong>Dry-rotted tires cannot be repaired.</strong> They need to be replaced before the cracks reach the steel belts underneath.</p>
<h3 class="text-text-100 mt-2 -mb-1 text-base font-bold">2. Bulges or Bubbles</h3>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">A visible bulge on the sidewall means the internal cords have separated — usually from a pothole hit, a curb strike, or heat fatigue. A tire with a bubble can fail <strong>without warning</strong> at highway speed. If you see one, don't drive the vehicle any further than your nearest tire shop in Ocala.</p>
<h3 class="text-text-100 mt-2 -mb-1 text-base font-bold">3. Tread Worn Below 4/32"</h3>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">The old penny test (Lincoln's head) tells you when a tire is legally bald at 2/32". But in Florida's daily summer thunderstorms, <strong>anything under 4/32" dramatically increases hydroplaning risk</strong>. Bring a quarter instead — if the tread doesn't reach Washington's head, your tires are ready to replace.</p>
<h3 class="text-text-100 mt-2 -mb-1 text-base font-bold">4. Uneven or Cupped Wear</h3>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">If one edge of your tire is worn more than the other, or you see scalloped "cupping" across the tread, you have an alignment, suspension, or balance issue. Continuing to drive on unevenly worn tires in the heat accelerates failure — and it's throwing away rubber you already paid for. A quick alignment check and tire rotation in Ocala can save the remaining tread life.</p>
<h3 class="text-text-100 mt-2 -mb-1 text-base font-bold">5. Vibration at Highway Speed</h3>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Heading up I-75 to Gainesville or down to Orlando? If the steering wheel shakes between 50 and 70 mph, you likely have a balance issue, a belt separation inside the tire, or a bent wheel. All three get worse in heat, and all three are dangerous.</p>
<h3 class="text-text-100 mt-2 -mb-1 text-base font-bold">6. Tire Pressure Light That Keeps Coming On</h3>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Florida's daily temperature swings — cool mornings, 95° afternoons — cause tire pressure to fluctuate 3–5 PSI per day. A TPMS light that turns on overnight but goes off by noon is usually normal. One that stays on, or returns within days of being topped off, means you have a slow leak, a failing valve stem, or a porous wheel. Don't ignore it.</p>
<h3 class="text-text-100 mt-2 -mb-1 text-base font-bold">7. Tires Older Than 6 Years (Regardless of Tread)</h3>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Find the DOT code on the sidewall. The last four digits are the week and year of manufacture — "2419" means the 24th week of 2019. In Florida's climate, <strong>tires older than six years should be replaced even if the tread looks good.</strong> The rubber compounds are no longer safe at highway speed, especially once summer pavement temperatures climb.</p>

<h2 class="text-text-100 mt-3 -mb-1 text-[1.125rem] font-bold">How to Make Your Tires Last Longer in Florida</h2>
<p class="font-claude-response-body break-words whitespace-normal leading-[1.7]">Even the best all-season tires wear out faster here than they would up north, but a few habits extend their life:</p>
<ul class="[li_&amp;]:mb-0 [li_&amp;]:mt-1 [li_&amp;]:gap-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ul]:pb-1 [&amp;:not(:last-child)_ol]:pb-1 list-disc flex flex-col gap-1 pl-8 mb-3">
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong><a href="http://tire-express.local/tire-balancing-and-rotation/">Rotate</a> every 5,000–7,500 miles.</strong> Front tires in Ocala wear faster because of the heat, braking, and steering load.</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Check pressure monthly.</strong> Underinflated tires build up heat fast — the #1 cause of summer blowouts.</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Park in the shade when you can.</strong> UV is the enemy. A carport or garage adds years of life.</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Don't skip the alignment.</strong> Florida's road construction and potholes knock cars out of alignment constantly. Alignment is the cheapest way to protect your tire investment.</li>
<li class="whitespace-normal break-words pl-2"><strong>Buy tires rated for hot climates.</strong> Not every "all-season" tire is designed for sustained high-heat driving. Ask about heat-rated touring and performance tires next time you shop.</li>
</ul>
