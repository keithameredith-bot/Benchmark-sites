What's Involved in a Brake Job?

There are two main components to a brake job: the brake pads and the rotors. Brakes work by compressing the pad into the rotor to create friction and slow the vehicle to a stop. Pads wear quicker than rotors — they're made of ceramic, organic, or semi-metallic material. Rotors are steel and connected to the wheels. When we do a brake job, we check all of these components to make sure everything is working the way it should.

When your brakes are in that middle ground — not failing, but not fresh — we'll tell you that too, so you can make the best decision for both your safety and your wallet.

Know the Signs

Do I Need Pads, Rotors, or Both?

Brake Warning Light

Newer vehicles have an indicator light that tells you it's time to get your brakes checked. Don't ignore it.

High-Pitched Squeal

Older vehicles have a metal wear clip that squeals when you brake — that's your car telling you it's time for new pads.

Squishy Pedal

If your brake pedal feels soft or squishy, come in for a free inspection — it's worth catching early.

Worn Rotors

Rotors worn too thin are a matter of time before something serious. We measure them and tell you exactly where they stand.

Warped Rotors

New pads on warped rotors won't last like they should — you end up spending more, sooner. We'll catch it before you do.

How long are brake pads good for?

Depending on your driving style, the type of pad, and your vehicle, brake pads typically last anywhere between 20,000 and 50,000 miles.

How long are rotors good for?

Similar to pads, it depends — but rotors are generally good for anywhere between 30,000 and 70,000 miles. If rotors are warped or worn too thin, new pads won't last like they should, so we always measure them as part of the inspection.

How do I know if I need a brake job?

Newer vehicles show a brake indicator light. Older vehicles have a metal clip that makes a high-pitched squeal when you brake. A soft or squishy pedal is another sign. Any of those — or any doubt at all — come by and we'll take a look for free.
